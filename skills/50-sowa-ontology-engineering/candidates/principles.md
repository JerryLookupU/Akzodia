# Candidate Principles

## Included Principles

1. Ontologies are for a language and purpose.
   - Use: require purpose, agent, and competency questions before category creation.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

2. Primitive categories are constrained by axioms and examples, not always closed-form definitions.
   - Use: record open assumptions instead of pretending the ontology is complete.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

3. The top-level pass must separate independent, relative, and mediating entities.
   - Use: prevent role, relation, situation, reason, and purpose from being modeled as ordinary objects.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

4. The physical/abstract distinction must separate runtime things from information structures.
   - Use: distinguish process run from workflow definition, log from process, schema from object.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

5. The continuant/occurrent distinction must separate identity through time from unfolding through time.
   - Use: distinguish object, process, history, stage, event, and state.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

6. The lexicon is a bridge, not a glossary.
   - Use: lexical entries must link word senses to grammar, semantic representation, background knowledge, and application purpose.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

7. Metadata must connect signs to objects and interpreting agents.
   - Use: reject metadata that only tags strings with more strings.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

8. Roles should be modeled as role types plus relations, not only ad hoc dyadic predicates.
   - Use: define role signatures and `Has`-style relations.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

9. Ambiguous participants should be represented at the most informative common supertype.
   - Use: generalize to `Product` or `Source` until evidence justifies `Result`, `Patient`, `Agent`, `Effector`, or `Instrument`.
   - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

10. Causal models are approximations for a purpose.
    - Use: model causal influence as state/event dependency without claiming ultimate causation.
    - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

11. Process types require distinctions beyond step order.
    - Use: check discreteness, branching, ramification, delay, concurrency, predictability, normality, hierarchy, time-boundedness, and memory.
    - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.

12. Be systematic but keep systems open.
    - Use: document unresolved primitives, irrelevant distinctions, and future expansion paths.
    - Sources: `references/source-notes.md`.
