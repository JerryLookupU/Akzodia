# Principle Candidates

## Accepted Principles

1. Start from the knowledge task, not the notation.
   - Evidence: preface emphasizes analyzing informal specifications and selecting an appropriate ontology.

2. Separate term, concept, relation, rule, context, source, and explanation.
   - Evidence: ontology and lexicon links on landing page; index entries for lexical/content words, concepts, roles, relation types, context, and source-linked subjects.

3. Treat logic as explanation and contradiction machinery.
   - Evidence: preface states logic supplies formal structure and inference; index includes rules of inference, soundness, completeness, proof, theorem proving.

4. Treat ontology as domain commitment.
   - Evidence: TOC chapter 2 and appendix B; index entries for ontological commitment, top-level categories, continuants, occurrents, roles.

5. Treat computation as a constraint.
   - Evidence: preface distinguishes KR from pure philosophy via computable models; TOC covers computation, constraints, sharing, and acquisition tools.

6. Make contexts explicit before mixing claims.
   - Evidence: chapter 5 and index entries for context syntax, semantics, modal and intentional contexts.

7. Represent change with process-aware structures.
   - Evidence: chapter 4 and index entries for Petri nets, situation calculus, preconditions, postconditions, persistence.

8. Do not flatten uncertainty.
   - Evidence: chapter 6 separates vagueness, uncertainty, randomness, ignorance, fuzzy logic, nonmonotonic logic, and semiotics.

9. Record semantic losses in translation.
   - Evidence: chapter 7 covers relating different representations and multiple paradigms; appendices compare notations.

10. Validate by elaboration tolerance.
   - Evidence: inferred from Sowa's comparative treatment and KR principle index entries; accepted because it predicts whether a representation survives new exceptions, contexts, and mappings.

## Merged Principles

- "Use conceptual graphs everywhere" was merged into representation selection. The source treats conceptual graphs as important, but not exclusive.
- "Use formal logic for all knowledge" was merged into the triad. Chapter 6 explicitly covers limitations of logic and adaptations to real-world complexity.
